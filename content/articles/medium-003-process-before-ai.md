# Stop Implementing AI. Start Analyzing Your Processes.

*Published in HARMONIC insight on Medium*

---

Last year, a mid-sized construction company called me in a panic. They had spent eight months and ¥60M implementing an AI-powered project management system. The tool was sophisticated. The vendor was reputable. The implementation was technically sound.

It was a disaster.

Projects were being flagged as "at risk" when they were fine. Resource allocation recommendations made no sense. The AI kept optimizing for metrics that had nothing to do with how the company actually delivered work.

The CEO asked me: "Is AI just not ready for construction?"

I asked him: "Before you bought this tool, did anyone map out how your project managers actually run projects?"

Silence.

This is the story I have seen play out over a hundred times in thirty years of consulting. And it is getting worse, not better, because the AI hype cycle is compressing the gap between "we should do something with AI" and "we just signed a ¥100M contract."

---

## The Pattern Nobody Talks About

Here is what I see, over and over, across industries and company sizes:

1. **Executive reads an article** about AI transforming their industry
2. **Company buys an AI or RPA tool** — usually the one with the best sales pitch
3. **IT department implements it** on existing processes
4. **The existing processes were broken** — but nobody knew, because nobody had analyzed them
5. **The AI faithfully automates the broken process** — faster, at scale, with no human judgment to catch the errors
6. **Results are terrible.** Costs go up. Errors multiply. Employees revolt.
7. **Executive declares: "AI doesn't work for our business"**

The AI worked perfectly. It did exactly what it was told to do. The problem is that nobody understood what it should have been told to do, because nobody understood the process it was supposed to improve.

I call this **automated chaos**. You took a mess and made it run at machine speed.

---

## Three Companies, Three Versions of the Same Mistake

**The Construction Company.** Their project managers had developed informal workarounds over fifteen years to deal with a rigid legacy system. Material ordering, subcontractor scheduling, progress reporting — none of it followed the "official" process documented in their ISO manuals. When the AI was trained on the official process, it produced outputs that were irrelevant to how work actually got done. Eight months and ¥60M to automate a process that did not exist in reality.

**The BPO Firm.** They implemented RPA to automate invoice processing. Forty bots running twenty-four hours a day. Throughput increased 300%. Error rate also increased 300%. Why? Because the original process had a step where a senior clerk would glance at an invoice and catch obvious mistakes — wrong vendor codes, duplicate entries, mismatched amounts. That step was not documented anywhere. It was institutional knowledge in one person's head. The bots did not know it existed. They processed garbage at extraordinary speed.

**The Manufacturer.** They deployed an AI quality inspection system on their production line. The system was trained on defect images and performed well in testing. In production, it flagged 40% of acceptable parts as defective. The reason: operators on the floor had been making micro-adjustments to tolerances based on material batch variations — adjustments that were never captured in any specification document. The AI was comparing against specs that no human actually followed.

Three different industries. Three different technologies. One identical root cause: **nobody analyzed the actual process before automating it.**

---

## Why This Keeps Happening

It is not stupidity. The executives at these companies are sharp. The IT teams are competent. The vendors are delivering real technology. So why does this pattern repeat?

**Because process analysis is unglamorous work.** It means sitting with front-line workers for weeks. It means documenting what people actually do, not what the procedure manual says they do. It means asking uncomfortable questions about why the official process and the real process diverged — and discovering that the real process is often better, developed through years of practical experience.

Nobody gets promoted for spending three months analyzing how invoices flow through an organization. People get promoted for implementing AI.

This is the fundamental misalignment. The work that matters most — understanding the process — is the work that gets skipped.

## The Right Approach: Process First, Technology Second

In 2022, I built a business process consulting team from zero. Twenty people, focused on one thing: understanding how organizations actually work before recommending any technology. By year three, that team was generating ¥450M in revenue. Not because process consulting is trendy — it is the opposite of trendy. Because it works.

Here is what we learned.

The correct sequence is:

1. **Analyze the process** — the real one, not the documented one
2. **Identify what is actually broken** — and what is working fine
3. **Fix the process itself first** — sometimes the answer is a better manual process
4. **Then, and only then, decide on technology** — AI, RPA, simple automation, or nothing at all
5. **Implement the technology on a clean, understood process**

Step 3 is where most companies skip ahead. They go from "we have a process" straight to "let's automate it." They miss the part where you make the process worth automating.

## A Practical Framework: Five Steps Before You Automate Anything

I have used this framework across more than a hundred engagements. It is not theoretical. It is what works.

### Step 1: Walk the Process, End to End

Do not read the documentation. Go to where the work happens. Sit with the people who do the work. Watch them. Ask them to narrate what they are doing and why. You will discover within the first hour that the documented process and the actual process are different. They always are.

Document what you see, not what you expected to see.

### Step 2: Find the Gaps Between "Official" and "Actual"

Map the gaps between the documented process and reality. Every gap is a signal. Some gaps exist because the official process is outdated. Some exist because employees found a better way. Some exist because there is a systemic problem nobody addressed.

Do not judge the gaps. Understand them.

### Step 3: Identify the Value Points and the Waste

Not every step in a process adds value. Some steps exist because of a problem that was solved three years ago. Some exist because a regulation changed and nobody updated the workflow. Some steps add enormous value but are invisible because they happen in someone's head.

Map which steps create value for the customer or the business. Map which steps are pure waste. Map which steps are workarounds for upstream failures.

### Step 4: Redesign the Process for Humans First

Before you introduce any technology, redesign the process as if you had to run it manually. Eliminate waste. Standardize the workarounds that work. Fix the upstream problems that create downstream workarounds. Make the process clean, logical, and efficient for humans.

This step alone — no technology involved — typically produces a 20-30% improvement.

### Step 5: Now Choose Your Technology

With a clean, understood, optimized process in hand, you can make an intelligent decision about technology:

- **Some steps are perfect for AI** — pattern recognition, prediction, natural language processing
- **Some steps are perfect for RPA** — high-volume, rule-based, repetitive tasks
- **Some steps are perfect for simple automation** — a script, a scheduled job, a form
- **Some steps should stay manual** — judgment calls, relationship work, creative decisions

The technology decision becomes obvious when you understand the process. When you do not understand the process, every technology decision is a guess.

## Why This Matters Right Now

We are in the middle of the largest technology spending wave in corporate history. Every company is being told they need AI. Vendors are selling AI solutions for every conceivable business problem. Consultants are building AI practices as fast as they can hire.

Most of this spending will be wasted.

Not because AI is bad technology. AI is extraordinary technology. But extraordinary technology applied to poorly understood processes produces extraordinarily expensive failures.

The companies that will win in this era are not the ones that implement AI the fastest. They are the ones that understand their processes deeply enough to implement AI where it actually matters — and have the discipline to fix their processes where AI is not the answer.

Speed of AI adoption is not a competitive advantage. Quality of process understanding is.
