# I Built a $3M Consulting Practice from Zero in 3 Years. Here's What Actually Worked.

*Published in HARMONIC insight on Medium*

---

In 2022, I was a team of one inside a large consulting firm. No clients. No revenue. No team. Three years later: 20 people, $3M in revenue, and our first profitable year.

This isn't a motivational story. It's a breakdown of what actually worked, what didn't, and what I'd do differently. If you're building a consulting practice — or any professional services business — the specifics matter more than the inspiration.

---

## Why I Started: The Gap Nobody Was Filling

I've spent 30 years in IT consulting. I've worked at firms like ISID (now dentsu SOKEN), PwC, and FPT. I've helped over 100 companies with digital transformation, enterprise architecture, and technology strategy.

And after all of that, I noticed a pattern that kept costing companies millions:

**IT consultants recommend technology. Business people understand operations. Almost nobody bridges the two.**

Here's what I saw, over and over:

1. A company decides they need AI, RPA, or some new system
2. They hire an IT consulting firm
3. The consultants jump straight to the technology — tool selection, architecture, implementation
4. The project goes live
5. Nothing changes. The business process was broken before the technology, and it's still broken after

The missing step? **Understanding the actual business process first.** Before you automate anything, you need to know what you're automating. Before you implement AI, you need to know what decisions are being made, by whom, with what data, and why.

This sounds obvious. It isn't. The consulting industry makes money selling technology implementations, not process analysis. Process work is slow, unglamorous, and hard to put on a slide deck. So nobody wants to do it.

I decided to build a team that would.

---

## Year 1 — The Hardest Year

In early 2022, I pitched the idea internally: a business process consulting practice within an IT consulting firm. The response was polite skepticism. Nobody said no, but nobody gave me a team either. I was told to prove the concept.

So I did. Alone.

**Finding the first client** was the hardest part. Our firm's existing clients expected IT consulting. When I told them I wanted to analyze their business processes before touching any technology, most of them looked confused. "We already know our processes," they'd say. "We just need the system."

They didn't know their processes. They never do. There's always a gap between how people think work gets done and how it actually gets done.

My first client was a mid-size company that had failed two consecutive IT projects. They'd spent significant budget on systems that nobody used, because the systems were designed for a process that didn't exist in reality. They were desperate enough to try something different.

I spent three months mapping their actual operations. Not the org chart version. Not the ISO documentation version. The real version — the workarounds, the Excel spreadsheets that held everything together, the decisions that happened in hallway conversations.

When I presented the findings, the CEO said something I'll never forget: "Nobody has ever shown us how our company actually works."

That project became my proof of concept. I delivered a process redesign that didn't require any new technology at all — just reorganized workflows and clear decision rules. The client saved time and money immediately.

Revenue in Year 1: Modest. But enough to prove the model worked.

---

## Year 2 — Building the Team

With one successful case study, everything changed.

**The case study sold better than any proposal ever could.** I stopped writing elaborate pitch decks and started telling the story of that first client. "Here's a company that spent millions on IT and got nothing. Here's what we found when we actually looked at their processes. Here's what changed."

That story opened doors. By mid-2023, I had more work than I could handle alone, and the firm gave me the green light to hire.

**Hiring was the second-hardest thing I did.** The obvious choice was to hire consultants — people with Big Four experience, MBA degrees, and polished presentation skills. I didn't do that.

Instead, I hired people who had actually worked inside businesses. Former operations managers. People who had run back-office teams. A few who had spent years in business process outsourcing. They understood what happens on the ground floor because they'd lived it.

This was a deliberate choice. You can teach someone consulting frameworks. You can't easily teach someone what it feels like to run a 50-person operations team and deal with broken processes every day.

By the end of Year 2, we were a team of about 10. Revenue was growing, but we weren't profitable yet. The overhead of ramping up people — training, bench time between projects, the cost of getting new hires productive — ate into margins.

What kept me going: every client engagement validated the model. Companies that had been burned by technology-first consulting were specifically seeking us out. Word was spreading.

---

## Year 3 — The ¥450M Milestone

2024 was the year it all came together.

**Revenue hit ¥450M — roughly $3M.** The team grew to 20 people. And for the first time, we were profitable.

What changed between Year 2 and Year 3 wasn't strategy. It was compounding.

**Repeat clients.** The companies we'd helped in Year 1 and Year 2 came back. Not because we upsold them — because they had new processes to fix. Once a company sees what real process analysis looks like, they can't go back to the old way.

**Referrals.** Our clients started recommending us to their partners, suppliers, and industry peers. One client referral was worth more than six months of business development.

**Speed.** Our team had done enough engagements that we could pattern-match. We'd seen the same broken processes across industries — procurement workflows that had 15 approval steps when 3 would do, reporting processes where the same data was manually entered into four different systems, decision-making bottlenecks where one person signed off on everything because nobody had defined delegation rules.

We weren't reinventing the wheel each time. We had frameworks, templates, and — most importantly — a library of real examples showing what good looks like.

**What didn't work:** Trying to sell process consulting as a standalone service to companies that weren't in pain. The clients who came to us after a failed IT project, after a reorganization that didn't deliver, after an automation initiative that automated the wrong things — those clients got it immediately. The ones who thought everything was fine? They didn't want to hear that their processes were broken.

So we stopped trying to convince the unconvinced and focused on the companies that already knew they had a problem.

## 5 Lessons I'd Pass On

### 1. Process before technology, always.

This is our entire value proposition, and it holds up every single time. I've never seen a company where the technology was the real problem. It's always the process underneath. AI won't fix a broken workflow. RPA won't help if you're automating a process that shouldn't exist. The boring work of mapping, analyzing, and redesigning business processes is where the actual value lives.

### 2. One successful case study is worth 100 proposals.

I spent weeks early on crafting detailed proposals and pitch presentations. They had a low conversion rate. Then I started leading with the story of one real client, with real numbers, and real outcomes. Conversion went through the roof. People don't buy methodology. They buy proof that you've done it before.

### 3. Hire people who understand the business, not just the tools.

The consulting industry is obsessed with certifications, frameworks, and tools. Those matter, but they're not differentiators. What's rare is someone who has sat in an operations room at 11 PM trying to close the month-end books, or who has managed a team doing manual data entry and knows exactly which steps can be eliminated. Hire from the business side, then teach them consulting. Not the other way around.

### 4. The consulting industry has a blind spot: nobody wants to do the boring process work.

Big firms want to sell transformation programs, digital strategies, and platform implementations. Those are high-margin, high-visibility projects. Sitting in a conference room for two weeks documenting how purchase orders actually get processed? That's not glamorous. But it's where you find the problems that, when fixed, make everything else work. The fact that nobody wants to do this work is exactly why there's so much opportunity in it.

### 5. Building software products on the side made me a better consultant (and vice versa).

Outside of my consulting practice, I build AI-powered software products solo. I've shipped 10 products, published 50 books, and have over 140 repositories on GitHub. This isn't a separate hobby — it's deeply connected.

Building software taught me to think in systems. When I map a client's business process, I'm thinking about it the way a software architect thinks about data flow — inputs, outputs, decision points, exceptions. That precision makes my process analysis sharper than what a traditional consultant would produce.

Going the other direction: 30 years of consulting taught me what real business problems look like. Every product I build solves a problem I've personally seen dozens of times. The consulting gives me problems worth solving. The product development gives me tools to think about those problems more clearly.

## What's Next

For three years, we've proven that process-first consulting works — in Japan. Every company we've helped has had the same realization: the technology wasn't the issue. The process was.

That problem isn't unique to Japan. It's everywhere.

Companies around the world are spending billions on AI, automation, and digital transformation without first understanding their own operations. The same gap I saw in 2022 exists in every market.

So that's the next chapter. Taking process-first thinking to international markets. Sharing the frameworks, the case studies, and the approach that turned a team of one into a profitable practice of 20.

The consulting industry doesn't need more technology experts. It needs people willing to do the hard, boring, essential work of understanding how businesses actually operate — and fixing that first.
