I Spent 28 Years Designing Systems I Couldn't Build. Then AI Changed That.


For 28 years, I designed systems I couldn't build.

Then AI changed that.

Now I build enterprise software alone.

By day, I'm a Managing Director at an IT consulting firm — ISID (now dentsu SOKEN), PwC, FPT. 28 years in the industry since 1997, over 100 companies. System development was my main work for most of that time, with about 10 years focused on consulting. In 2022, I launched a new consulting practice from zero inside the firm — no clients, no team, no revenue. I personally won every deal. Three years later: 20 people, ¥450M ($3M) in annual revenue, and our first profitable year.

By night — and weekends, and early mornings — I build software products. By myself. With AI.

No development team. No venture capital. No startup office. Just me, Claude, and 28 years of knowing exactly what businesses actually need.

This is the story of how it happened — step by step.


10 Years of Compromise

For the first decade of my career, I designed enterprise accounting packages. I wrote the specifications, managed the roadmap. But the actual code was in the hands of programmers.

Every decision I cared about — UI, workflow, usability — was slightly wrong. And I couldn't fix it.

The screen layout I envisioned? Slightly different. The workflow I designed? Implemented with shortcuts I didn't agree with. The small UI fixes I requested? "Too expensive to change."

I accepted it. For 10 years.

After that, I spent nearly two decades in system development and consulting — designing enterprise systems, leading DX projects, advising executives. The gap between my vision and the delivered product never went away. I just learned to manage it.

Then, in 2025, everything changed.


50 Stories and the 24-Hour AI Workforce

It started with a simple realization: I'm paying a monthly subscription for AI, and it sits idle while I sleep. That's 8 hours of wasted capacity every night.

So I put it to work.

In early 2025, I started an experiment. Every night before bed, I'd give the AI a task: research what genres are trending in the Japanese novel market, analyze what readers want, propose story concepts, and in some cases, write the draft. By morning, I'd have material to review, edit, and publish.

This is how I published 36 books and 15 short stories on Kindle. Alone. With AI. While sleeping.

But the more I did it, the more I learned something important: AI is fast, but it's shallow. The plots were technically competent but lacked depth. The characters were functional but not interesting. AI doesn't have lived experience, so its stories feel generic.

That's when I shifted. Instead of letting AI choose what to write, I started writing about things I actually cared about — architecture, the future of cities, construction, technology. Stories with substance that came from my own knowledge and curiosity. AI became the executor, not the thinker.

This same principle now drives how I build software. With Claude Code on the Max Plan, I run development cycles around the clock. During the day, I use the products myself and evaluate them. At night, AI builds. My role is simple: I define two things — features that sell, and features that make the product easy to use. Then I evaluate, give feedback, and loop. Evaluate, feedback, loop. Over and over.

The AI never sleeps. And neither does the product development.

I also needed a platform to publish the novels — something I could control. So I built that too. It had been years since I'd written code, and back then AI coding tools were limited. It was slow and painful, but it worked.

And it reawakened something. The urge to build.


From Android Apps to Desktop Software

First, I built Android apps — frustrated by ads, paywalls, and apps that didn't do what I needed. I started with Android Studio, copying and pasting code, debugging manually. Then I discovered GitHub CI/CD and Claude Code. Development went from painful to fun.

Then came the real breakthrough: Claude Code CLI. Winter 2025.

For the first time, I could build full desktop applications locally — real, installable, production-quality Windows software.

15 years ago, I had designed accounting packages and logistics systems. I knew every business rule, every edge case, every workflow. But I couldn't build them myself — I needed a team of 20 developers.

Now, with Claude Code CLI, C#, and WPF, I could build those same applications. Alone.

This was the moment the 28-year gap disappeared.

The first time I saw a simple Python automation script reborn as a full C#/WPF desktop application with a professional UI, I felt something I hadn't felt in years.

Not satisfaction. Not pride.

Awe.


What I Built: The Insight Business Suite

Once I realized what was possible, I couldn't stop. Every product in the suite comes from a real problem I'd witnessed — and been unable to solve — during my career.

Every product solves a problem I personally experienced.

Insight Training Studio
Japan's construction industry faces chronic labor shortages and an aging workforce. At the end of every reform project, the answer is always education and training. But today's generation learns from video, not manuals. So I built a tool that converts PowerPoint decks — the training materials every company already has — into narrated training videos. 3 minutes. 13 languages. 100% local.

It also captures the knowledge that experienced workers carry in their heads before they retire — collecting, structuring, and converting it into training content.

Insight AI Office
Most AI hype is glorified full-text search. What business users actually need is to work with their Excel, Word, and PowerPoint files — faster and smarter. AI Office opens and edits all major formats in one tool, with embedded AI prompts that operate directly on your data. Extract rows from 50 Excel files. Clean up 200 Word documents. Write a prompt, and it executes.

Built for companies where corporate security policies block ChatGPT and Copilot — but who desperately need AI capabilities.

Insight Deck Quality Gate
Senior consultants spend hours reviewing decks — checking formatting, consistency, logical flow. This review process is mechanical enough to automate but important enough that you can't skip it. It compares slides, detects inconsistencies, and flags issues — so consultants can focus on the thinking, not the formatting.

Insight Performance Management
Years ago, I built a consolidated accounting and Excel version management system for a client. The project cost ¥3.4 billion ($23M). It tracked change history across Excel files and handled consolidated financial reporting.

That system is now in my product suite — rebuilt by one person using AI. The same logic, implemented in C#/WPF.

10 products in total. All desktop applications. All running locally. All built by one person.


Why Local-First?

Most AI tools are disqualified from enterprise use. Not because they are bad. Because they require data to leave the company.

Every product I build runs on your machine. No cloud. No per-user subscription. No data leaves your computer.

Security: Construction companies, financial institutions, government agencies — their data cannot go to a third-party cloud. Every SaaS AI tool that requires cloud upload is disqualified from 40% of the enterprise market.

Cost: Per-user SaaS pricing kills enterprise adoption. My tools use a flat pricing model — no per-user fees, no escalating costs as your team grows.

Dependency: I've seen companies build their entire workflow on a SaaS product, only to face a 3x price increase. Local software you own doesn't disappear.


150 Repositories and Counting

Today, I have over 150 GitHub repositories. Every day, I'm testing new ideas with AI, building prototypes, pushing boundaries.

And the more I build, the more clearly I see the truth about AI-assisted development:

AI gets you to 80%. The last 20% is yours.

AI can write the code. It can generate the UI. It can implement the logic. But it cannot tell you whether the product feels right. It cannot judge whether the user experience is intuitive. It cannot sense whether the performance is acceptable for a real workflow.

In Japan, there's a tradition called "maneki no me-ire" — painting the eyes on a daruma doll. The doll is complete in every way except the eyes. Without them, it's just a shape. The eyes bring it to life.

AI builds the doll. I paint the eyes.

This is what 28 years of experience gives you. Not the ability to write code — AI does that now. The ability to know what "good" looks like. To feel when something is wrong before you can articulate why. To evaluate a product the way an end user would, not the way a developer would.

For decades, whenever a client described their requirements, I'd listen carefully — and then design something different. Not what they asked for. What I believed they actually needed. I designed systems as if I were going to use them myself. Every screen, every workflow, every button placement — I'd ask: "Would I be satisfied with this?"

Because when you build software for yourself, you can't hide from bad design. You use it every day, and if it's bad, you feel it every day. This is why my products are different from most enterprise software. They're not built to match a specification. They're built to be used.

I am not a programmer. I am an architect. A designer. An artist. The AI is my instrument.
