# Why Vibe Coding Fails in Enterprise — And What to Do Instead

*Published on note.com — HARMONIC insight (English)*

---

Last year, "vibe coding" was named Collins Dictionary's Word of the Year. By 2026, 92% of US developers use AI coding tools daily. Gartner predicts 60% of new code will be AI-generated by year-end.

The hype is real. But so are the failures.

I've spent 28 years building enterprise systems for over 100 companies — construction, manufacturing, logistics, finance. I've seen what works and what doesn't when technology meets real business operations.

And what I'm seeing now with vibe coding in enterprise settings is a pattern I've seen before: **the tool is powerful, but the process is missing.**

---

## The Promise vs. The Reality

Here's what vibe coding promises:

> "Just describe what you want in plain English, and AI writes the code."

For a weekend side project? That works beautifully. I've built prototypes in hours that would have taken weeks.

But for enterprise internal applications — the ones that run your business — the story is very different.

### What actually happens in enterprise vibe coding:

**Week 1:** The team is excited. A developer prompts Claude or Cursor, and a working prototype appears in two hours. Everyone says, "This is the future."

**Week 3:** The prototype has grown into a tangled mess. Nobody documented what was built or why. The AI generated 15,000 lines of code that nobody fully understands.

**Week 6:** A new requirement comes in. The developer prompts the AI to add a feature, and it breaks three existing ones. "Just regenerate it" becomes the default fix.

**Week 10:** The project is abandoned. The team goes back to their SI vendor and pays $50,000 for a "proper" system.

Sound familiar?

---

## The Three Failure Modes

After watching dozens of enterprise teams attempt vibe coding, I've identified three consistent failure patterns.

### Failure Mode 1: No Source, No Structure

Most teams jump straight to prompting the AI without understanding their own data.

They ask the AI to "build an inventory management system" without first answering:

- Where does the data currently live? (Excel? Paper? Someone's head?)
- Who inputs the data? Who consumes it?
- What's the current workflow, and which parts actually need software?

**The AI can write code. It cannot understand your business.**

In my experience across 100+ system implementations, 80% of project failures trace back to this: the team didn't understand their own information sources before they started building.

### Failure Mode 2: No Incremental Validation

Traditional development has a concept called "iterative delivery." Vibe coding teams often skip it entirely.

They prompt the AI to build the entire application at once:

> "Build me a complete asset management system with user authentication, role-based access control, Excel import/export, dashboard with KPIs, and audit logging."

The AI dutifully generates thousands of lines of code. It even runs. But it doesn't match what the users actually need — because nobody showed it to the users until it was "done."

**The fix isn't less AI. It's more structure around when and how you use it.**

### Failure Mode 3: No Separation of Concerns

Vibe coding makes it easy to mix everything together. Business logic, UI, database queries, authentication — all in one file, because "the AI just put it there."

Six months later, when you need to change the access control logic, you discover it's scattered across 47 files with no clear pattern. The AI that wrote it can't reliably modify it, because the codebase has no architecture.

Enterprise applications live for 5-10 years. The first version is just the beginning. **If you can't maintain it, you can't use it.**

## What Enterprise Vibe Coding Actually Needs

The solution isn't to abandon AI coding. The solution is to give it structure.

Here's what I've found works, after building enterprise systems for nearly three decades and applying AI-assisted development for the last two years:

### 1. Start with the Source, not the code

Before you write a single prompt, map your information sources:

- What Excel files, paper forms, or manual processes currently exist?
- Who creates the data? Who uses it? How does it flow?
- What decisions depend on this data?

This is what we call **"Source-First"** thinking. The AI doesn't need to understand your business — but *you* do, and you need to communicate it clearly.

### 2. Build in 10 small steps, not 1 big leap

Don't ask the AI to build everything at once. Instead:

1. Set up the project structure
2. Create a prioritized backlog (in Excel — your users already know Excel)
3. Define the menu structure with stakeholders
4. Design screens *before* writing code
5. Start with Excel upload → data display (prove the data flow works)
6. Build features in priority order, validating each one
7. Migrate to a proper database when the logic is proven
8. Incorporate user feedback systematically
9. Add access control based on validated requirements
10. Production deployment with authentication

Each step has a clear input, process, and output. Each step gets validated by real users before moving on. **The AI accelerates each step. The methodology ensures they happen in the right order.**

### 3. Separate what changes from what doesn't

Enterprise applications have parts that change frequently (business rules, UI) and parts that should be stable (authentication, data layer, access control).

Structure your AI prompts and your codebase to respect this boundary. When the AI generates code, put it in the right place — not wherever the AI decides to put it.
