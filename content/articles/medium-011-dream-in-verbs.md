# "What Do You Want to Be When You Grow Up?" Is the Wrong Question for 2050.

*Published in HARMONIC insight on Medium*

---

**TL;DR:** Job titles are artifacts of organizational structure, and organizational structures are being dismantled by AI. By 2050, many of today's job titles will not exist. But the verbs behind them — design, heal, protect, teach, build, analyze — are permanent. Career planning should start with verbs, not nouns.

---

## The Question That Broke

Every child hears it. "What do you want to be when you grow up?"

Doctor. Lawyer. Engineer. Firefighter. Pilot.

The question trains us from childhood to think about careers as nouns — job titles, fixed identities, things you become and remain. It worked reasonably well for a century in which most job titles were stable across an entire career. Your grandfather could be an "accountant" for forty years and the core of the job would be recognizable from start to finish.

That model is over.

In the past five years alone, entire job categories have been created (prompt engineer, AI safety researcher, drone fleet coordinator) and others have begun to dissolve (data entry clerk, basic translator, junior copywriter). The pace is accelerating. By 2050, projections suggest that 40–60% of current job titles will either not exist, will have been redefined beyond recognition, or will have been absorbed into broader role descriptions that bear no resemblance to today's categories.

If you plan your career around a job title, you are building on ground that is actively shifting beneath you.

---

## Why Job Titles Exist (And Why They Are Becoming Obsolete)

Job titles are organizational technology. They emerged during the Industrial Revolution as companies scaled beyond the point where everyone knew what everyone else did. Titles created legibility: they told the organization what a person was responsible for, and they told the person what was expected of them.

This worked because organizations were structured around stable, repeatable processes. An "accounts payable clerk" existed because there was a stable, well-defined accounts payable process that required a dedicated human to execute it. The title described the process. The person filled the title.

AI breaks this model in two ways.

**First, AI eliminates process-defined roles.** When AI handles the accounts payable process end-to-end, there is no role for an "accounts payable clerk" to fill. The process still happens. The title does not.

**Second, AI creates hybrid, fluid roles.** The work that remains after AI handles routine processes is judgment work, creative work, and relationship work — and this work does not fit neatly into a single title. A person might spend Monday doing strategic financial analysis, Tuesday facilitating a client negotiation, Wednesday designing a new workflow, and Thursday mentoring junior staff. What is their title? There is no noun that captures this.

Organizations are already moving toward titles that are vague by design: "business strategist," "solutions architect," "transformation lead." These titles do not describe a process. They describe a collection of capabilities. They are the transitional forms between the old model and whatever comes next.

## Verbs Are Permanent

Job titles come and go. But the underlying human activities — the verbs — persist across centuries.

**Heal.** Doctors, nurses, therapists, counselors, pharmacists, paramedics. The titles change. The organizations change. The tools change radically. But the activity of healing — restoring health, alleviating suffering, preventing disease — is constant.

**Teach.** Professors, tutors, trainers, coaches, mentors, curriculum designers. The delivery mechanisms have shifted from oral tradition to printed books to classrooms to online platforms to AI-assisted personalized learning. The verb endures.

**Build.** Architects, engineers, contractors, developers, designers. Whether the material is stone, steel, code, or data, the activity of taking a vision and making it real is permanent.

**Protect.** Soldiers, police, firefighters, cybersecurity analysts, compliance officers, insurance underwriters. The threats evolve. The methods evolve. The activity of protecting people, assets, and systems does not.

**Design.** Graphic designers, UX designers, architects, product designers, fashion designers, urban planners. Tools change from pencils to CAD to AI-generated prototypes. The activity of envisioning and shaping how things look, feel, and function persists.

**Analyze.** Scientists, researchers, analysts, investigators, diagnosticians. The data sources expand from handwritten ledgers to real-time global data streams processed by AI. The human activity of making sense of information and drawing conclusions remains essential.

These verbs are not tied to specific technologies, organizational structures, or economic models. They describe what humans do, not what organizations call them. And that is precisely why they are the right foundation for career planning in an era of radical uncertainty.

## Dream in Verbs: A Framework

If job titles are unreliable guides to the future, how do you plan a career? Here is a framework I have developed through analyzing 25 years of labor market shifts and projecting forward to 2050.

### Step 1: Identify Your Core Verbs

Ask yourself: what do I actually do, stripped of all organizational context? Not your title, not your department, not your company. What verbs describe the work that energizes you and that you are genuinely good at?

A "marketing manager" might discover that their core verbs are **persuade** and **design.** A "software engineer" might find that their verbs are **build** and **solve.** A "human resources director" might realize their verbs are **develop** (people) and **mediate.**

Most professionals have two to three core verbs. These are your career constants.

### Step 2: Separate Verbs from Current Delivery Mechanisms

Your core verbs are currently expressed through a specific set of tools, platforms, and organizational structures. Those will change. The verbs will not.

If your verb is **teach**, you might currently express it through classroom training. By 2035, you might express it through AI-personalized learning design. By 2050, through immersive experiential education platforms that do not exist yet. The verb stays. The delivery mechanism transforms.

The error is becoming attached to the delivery mechanism and mistaking it for the verb. "I am a classroom trainer" is a delivery mechanism identity. "I teach" is a verb identity. One is fragile. The other is resilient.

### Step 3: Build Verb-Portable Skills

Once you know your core verbs, invest in skills that make those verbs more powerful regardless of the delivery mechanism.

If your verb is **design:**
- Visual thinking (portable across every design medium)
- Systems thinking (understanding how components interact)
- User empathy (understanding what people need, not what they say they want)
- AI-augmented prototyping (the current and future delivery mechanism)

If your verb is **heal:**
- Diagnostic reasoning (portable across all medical technologies)
- Patient communication (irreplaceable by AI)
- Evidence evaluation (knowing which data to trust)
- AI-assisted treatment planning (the emerging delivery mechanism)

These skills compound across career transitions because they serve the verb, not the title.

### Step 4: Let Titles Find You

When you are organized around verbs and verb-portable skills, job titles become descriptive labels rather than identity anchors. You do not pursue a title. You pursue opportunities to exercise your verbs at higher levels of complexity and impact. The titles follow.

A person whose verb is **build** might hold titles of software developer, product manager, startup founder, and venture advisor across a thirty-year career. The titles change. The verb deepens. Each role develops the verb at a higher level.

## What Disappears, What Remains

By 2050, many specific job titles will be historical artifacts. But let us look at what stays:

| Verb | 2025 Titles | 2050 Titles (Projected) | The Constant |
|---|---|---|---|
| Design | UX Designer, Architect, Product Designer | Experience Architect, Environment Designer, AI-Human Interaction Designer | Creating things that work for humans |
| Heal | Doctor, Nurse, Therapist | Integrated Health Strategist, AI-Augmented Clinician, Wellness Architect | Restoring and maintaining human health |
| Teach | Professor, Trainer, Tutor | Learning Experience Designer, Cognitive Development Specialist, Knowledge Architect | Helping humans develop capabilities |
| Protect | Cybersecurity Analyst, Compliance Officer, Insurance Underwriter | Risk Ecosystem Manager, Digital Trust Architect, Resilience Designer | Keeping people and systems safe |
| Build | Software Engineer, Construction Manager, Entrepreneur | Systems Builder, Reality Architect, Autonomous Operations Designer | Turning vision into functional reality |

The titles on the right may sound speculative. They are. But the verbs in the left column are certain. Whatever the titles become, the underlying human activities persist.

## The Career Conversation We Need

We need to change how we talk to young people — and to ourselves — about careers.

Instead of "What do you want to be?" ask **"What do you want to do?"**

Instead of "What is your job title?" ask **"What verbs describe your best work?"**

Instead of "What career should I choose?" ask **"Which verbs energize me, and where can I exercise them at the highest level of impact?"**

This is not semantic wordplay. It is a fundamental reorientation from a noun-based career model (title, identity, stability) to a verb-based career model (activity, capability, adaptability). The noun model worked in a stable labor market. The verb model works in a transforming one.

---

## The Verb Test

Here is a practical exercise. Take five minutes right now.

1. Write down your current job title.
2. Below it, write the three to five verbs that describe what you actually do every day.
3. Cross out the title.
4. Look at the verbs.

Those verbs are your career. Everything else — the title, the company, the industry, the specific tools — is context. Context changes. Verbs endure.

Plan accordingly.
